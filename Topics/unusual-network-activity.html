Unusual Network Activity

Overview

Unusual network activity refers to deviations from established traffic baselines. This can include unexpected surges in data volume, communication with rare external destinations, or the use of unauthorized protocols. Identifying these anomalies is key to detecting lateral movement, data exfiltration, and command-and-control (C2) activity.

Attack Description

Adversaries often generate unusual network patterns when executing their mission. Common indicators include:

  • Exfiltration: Large outbound transfers to cloud storage or unfamiliar IPs.
  • Lateral Movement: Internal scanning or RDP/SSH connections between atypical workstations.
  • Beaconing: Consistent, periodic heartbeats to external C2 infrastructure.
  • Discovery: High volumes of "Connection Refused" or "Destination Unreachable" errors.

Investigation Workflow

Characterize the Activity

Type of unusual activity:

  • Volume Spike: Measure increase percentage and duration compared to the 7-day average.
  • Unusual Timing: Note deviation from the system's standard operating hours (e.g., midnight backups).
  • New Destination: Identify the target IP/Domain and its intended purpose.
  • New Protocol: Verify legitimacy (e.g., sudden use of FTP or Telnet in a secure zone).
  • Bandwidth Consumption: Quantify the data transfer to check for potential exfiltration.

Identify Source and Destination

  • Internal to Internal?
    • Check for signs of lateral movement.
    • Verify both source and destination systems for signs of compromise.
    • Review the relationship between systems (e.g., should a HR laptop be talking to a SQL Server?).
  • Internal to External?
    • Check the reputation of the external IP or domain via Threat Intel.
    • Review for data exfiltration indicators (e.g., long-lived connections, large uploads).
    • Verify the business need for this specific external communication.
  • External to Internal?
    • Verify the legitimacy of the inbound connection (e.g., VPN, Public Web Server).
    • Check for scan/probe activity (e.g., many ports hit in a short time).
    • Review firewall rules to ensure the traffic is authorized.

Compare to Baseline

Is there a legitimate reason for change?

  • New Application Deployment: Verify with the IT or Development team.
  • Business Process Change: Verify with the relevant business unit.
  • Scheduled Backup/Update: Check against known maintenance windows.
  • System Migration: Verify via an active Change Management ticket.
  • No Known Reason: Proceed to Malicious Indicator Analysis.

Does activity show malicious indicators?

  • Yes (C2 beaconing, exfiltration patterns, scanning)
    • Isolate affected systems immediately.
    • Capture network traffic (PCAP) for deep packet analysis.
    • Escalate to IR immediately.
    • Begin containment procedures (block IPs at the firewall).
  • Suspicious but unclear
    • Request Senior Analyst review.
    • Continue enhanced monitoring of the involved assets.
    • Analyze protocols and payloads if unencrypted.
    • Check for related alerts on the same hosts (e.g., AV or EDR alerts).
  • Benign
    • Update the behavioral baseline to include this activity.
    • Document the new "normal" behavior in the asset inventory.
    • Close the alert.

Tools & Queries

  • NetFlow / IPFIX Analysis Tools
  • Wireshark / Zeek (for traffic inspection)
  • Threat Intelligence (VirusTotal, Cisco Talos, Joe Sandbox)
  • SIEM Traffic Dashboards (Top Talkers, Byte Count spikes)

Response Workflow

  • Determine if the traffic represents a threat or a configuration change.
  • Block malicious external IPs at the perimeter firewall/proxy.
  • Isolate internal hosts showing signs of lateral movement or C2.
  • Update monitoring rules to account for authorized baseline shifts.
